摘  要
本文研究资本市场自由化是否会因果性地促进企业数字化转型。以中国A股公司分阶段纳入MSCI新兴市场指数为准自然实验，基于2010-2022年35,264个公司-年份观测值，构建双重差分（DID）识别框架，并结合倾向得分匹配（PSM）进行稳健性检验。数字化转型指标基于年报文本的计算文本分析方法构建，覆盖人工智能、大数据、云计算、物联网、区块链与企业数字化等六类技术领域。
实证结果表明，在基准设定下，MSCI纳入使企业数字化转型强度提高0.39个对数点，约为处理前水平的48%。事件研究结果显示处理前不存在系统性趋势差异，处理后效应持续存在。安慰剂检验、错位处理诊断、披露长度控制以及替代性指标检验均支持结论稳健。异质性分析显示，效应在高技术行业和经济较发达地区更为显著。机制检验基于六个代理变量，对融资约束缓解与公司治理改善两条渠道提供一致支持。总体而言，研究表明国际金融一体化可在新兴市场中发挥企业数字化升级的制度催化作用。
关键词：资本市场自由化；数字化转型；MSCI纳入；中国A股；新兴市场；计算文本分析；双重差分

 
Abstract
This thesis investigates whether capital market liberalization can causally promote digital transformation at the firm level within an emerging-market context. The empirical setting involves the phased inclusion of Chinese A-share firms into the MSCI Emerging Markets Index, serving as a policy-relevant institutional shock that increased the exposure of selected firms to global investors. Building upon this quasi-natural experiment, the study integrates an identification-oriented econometric methodology with comprehensive computational measurement to generate evidence that is both causally interpretable and substantively significant. Specifically, the analysis employs a panel comprising 35,264 firm-year observations from 2,712 listed firms spanning the years 2010 to 2022 and estimates treatment effects utilizing a two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences framework, complemented by event-study dynamics, matching-based diagnostics, and placebo-based falsification.
A central contribution of this thesis is the development of a comprehensive digital transformation outcome metric derived from Chinese annual report text. The metric is constructed employing natural language processing techniques and domain-specific dictionaries encompassing six technology clusters: artificial intelligence, big data analytics, cloud computing, Internet of Things, blockchain/distributed ledger technologies, and enterprise digitization initiatives. To mitigate concerns regarding keyword inflation and disclosure bias, the study validates the text-based indicators against external technology-related outcomes, applies logarithmic transformations and winsorization procedures, and conducts robustness tests using length-normalized outcomes and alternative index formulations. This measurement strategy facilitates consistent tracking of digital transformation intensity at the firm-year level in a context where direct accounting proxies are often incomplete or inconsistently comparable.
The baseline results demonstrate that MSCI inclusion enhances digital transformation intensity by 0.39 log points within the preferred specification, which is approximately a 48% increase relative to pre-treatment levels. Dynamic event-study estimates show no systematic divergence prior to treatment and indicate enduring post-treatment effects, thereby supporting the parallel-trends assumption and suggesting a cumulative adjustment process rather than a one-period reporting response. An extensive array of robustness checks, including placebo treatment assignments, staggered adoption diagnostics, propensity-score-matched DID estimation, alternative clustering strategies, restricted-sample tests, and multiple dependent-variable definitions, substantiate this conclusion. Throughout these analyses, the direction and substantive interpretation of the treatment effect remain consistent.
The thesis further explores heterogeneity and mechanistic pathways to transcend average effects. Heterogeneity analyses indicate more pronounced effects in high-technology industries and economically advanced regions, suggesting that absorptive capacity and institutional complementarity influence the benefits of financial liberalization. Evidence of mechanisms consistently supports two directly testable channels: alleviation of financing constraints and enhancement of governance quality through intensified external oversight and disclosure discipline. The knowledge spillover channel is theoretically incorporated and discussed as a significant extension; wherein more comprehensive mediator data would facilitate more precise direct testing. Overall, the study demonstrates that international financial integration can serve as an institutional catalyst for corporate digital upgrading in emerging markets. The findings contribute to three scholarly literatures: capital market liberalization, firm digital transformation, and computational corporate measurement. From a policy perspective, the results suggest that market-opening reforms can yield technology-upgrading benefits when supported by credible governance frameworks, transparent disclosure systems, and complementary regional capabilities. For corporations, the evidence indicates that enhanced access to global capital and oversight can substantially increase the feasibility and sustainability of long-term digital investment initiatives.
Key Words: Capital market liberalization; Digital transformation; MSCI inclusion; Chinese A-shares; Emerging markets; Computational text analysis; Difference-in-differences
 
Chapter 1	Introduction
This chapter introduces the core research problem, motivates its academic and policy relevance, and defines the thesis objectives and scope. It positions the study at the intersection of capital market liberalization and firm-level digital transformation, clarifies why the MSCI A-share inclusion episode provides a suitable quasi-experimental setting, and outlines the empirical and methodological contributions developed in subsequent chapters. The chapter also establishes the thesis's logical architecture so that the later identification, measurement, and interpretation sections can be read as parts of a single coherent causal argument.
Beyond orientation, the chapter specifies the analytical stance adopted throughout the thesis: digital transformation is treated as an investment process shaped not only by internal managerial capability but also by external capital-market institutions. This framing is important because it shifts the research question from whether firms transform to how financial opening changes the conditions under which transformation becomes feasible, credible, and sustained. By making this conceptual move explicit at the outset, the chapter prepares the reader for the integrated treatment of theory, institutional context, measurement design, and causal estimation that follows.
1.1	Research Background
The relationship between capital market liberalization and corporate digital transformation is currently a hot topic, especially in development economics, corporate finance, and technology management. Over the last two decades, governments in emerging nations have gradually opened their domestic financial institutions to international investors. At the same time, firms have been under increasing pressure to digitize production, operations, governance, and customer interactions. Despite the importance of these themes, prior research has often explored them separately rather than together. Financial liberalization research typically focuses on valuation implications, liquidity, and investment efficiency, whereas digital transformation research focuses on internal skills, leadership, and technology readiness Fang and Ju (2024). The critical question at the intersection of these literatures remains unanswered: can international capital market integration promote firm-level digital transformation, and if so, through which mechanisms?
This thesis is driven by the fundamental question concerning boundaries. It states that digital transformation involves both a financing and governance process, shaped by capital market institutions, as well as a management or technological process. Characteristics of digital transformation programs in many emerging markets include high fixed costs, uncertain returns, prolonged gestation periods, and strong complementarities with management learning Chanias et al. (2019). Even when expected social benefits are substantial, these features may introduce funding frictions that potentially hinder or delay adoption Chen & Zhang (2024). Capital markets liberalization might alter the investment considerations of firms contemplating digital upgrades by expanding the investor base, decreasing financing costs, and improving governance Barro (1990). Gaining a comprehensive understanding of this connection is essential for both scholarly inquiry and policy making.
The Chinese background is especially important for this research. China features scale, diversity, and institutional growth in ways that make it ideal for empirical study. Allen et al. (2017)It is one of the world's largest stock markets and a leading hub for industrial digitalization, yet capital account liberalization has mostly been gradual and driven by policy. P. Li et al. (2023)This slow process results in notable policy events with predictable timing. Among these, the inclusion of Chinese A-share firms in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index stands out as a key externally imposed reform that increased firms' exposure to international investors.
The practical reason behind this study is equally strong. Policymakers increasingly view digital transformation as a way to boost productivity and national competitiveness. However, firms vary greatly in their capacity to carry out transformation initiatives Liu et al. (2023). Market-opening policies may produce wider technical spillovers than are typically acknowledged if access to global capital and investor networks significantly influences digital adoption Zheng et al. (2021). On the other hand, liberalization may increase regional and firm-level disparities in digital capabilities if gains are concentrated among firms that already have advantages. Therefore, a thorough empirical evaluation is required.
Another important motivator is the measurement technique. A large portion of the research on digital transformation is based on managerial evaluations, survey data, or limited proxies like IT spending and patent counts. These metrics frequently have poor comparability over time, self-reporting bias, and restricted coverage Wang et al. (2022). In order to create a firm-year digital transformation index from annual report disclosures, this thesis uses a computational text-analysis approach. The project aims to generate evidence that is both strong in identification and wide in coverage by combining this large-scale measuring technique with a quasi-experimental methodology.
According to Chen et al. (2022), increased analyst coverage and enhanced information accuracy constitute two mechanisms through which digital transformation augments information environments. Cheng & Masron (2023)indicate that regulatory uncertainty can amplify enterprises' digital responses, thereby underscoring the significant influence of external environments on transformation behavior. Du & Wang (2024) find that the innovative benefits of digital transformation are substantially strengthened by financial support, particularly via capital markets, with effects that are statistically significant at the 1% level. WhileZhang & Wang (2024) some studies have found reduced equity financing costs for firms that have undergone digital transformation, Xue & Zhang (2024)others contend that digital transformation enhances debt financing outcomes and overall firm performance. Collectively, these findings suggest that the financial outcomes of digital transformation are largely contingent on specific conditions, disclosure quality, and governance frameworks.
While moving beyond correlation-based inference, this study builds on previous research. Most earlier studies focus on a single channel or outcome variable, often without addressing treatment assignment endogeneity. In contrast, this thesis combines mechanism-oriented research, a long panel (2010-2022), and a quasi-experimental policy shock to evaluate whether liberalization can be considered a causal factor in the level of digital transformation. The theory conceptualizes digital transformation as a process of cumulative strategic investment. Over time, firms adjust governance procedures, assess financing constraints, and update expectations. At least three mechanisms are expected for liberalization to influence this process: removing financial restrictions, enhancing governance and transparency standards through international oversight, and expanding access to cross-border knowledge networks. These mechanisms form the foundation of the thesis's empirical framework.
As a result, there are three main motivations. First, it tackles a significant open question in the literature about the relationship between firm-level technological innovation and global finance. Secondly, it offers evidence from a top and emerging economy that is methodologically robust. Third, it provides firms and regulators looking to integrate capital market reform with digital development goals with policy-relevant insight.
1.2	Research Questions and Objectives
In light of the aforementioned motivation, this thesis addresses three relevant research problems.
•	First, does inclusion in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index, a proxy for capital market liberalization, considerably raise the intensity of digital transformation at the firm level? The main causal question is this one. In comparison to similar untreated organizations, the study assesses whether treated firms see a consistent post-inclusion boost in digital transformation.
•	Second, do treatment effects vary by technology domain, industry, area, and firm characteristics? Digital transformation is not a single technology, but a collection of investments, each with its own capital intensity and capabilities requirements. The thesis investigates if effects are stronger in domains such as AI, big data, and cloud computing; whether reactions differ across technology-intensive and traditional industries; and whether regional institutional quality influences outcomes.
•	Third, how does liberalization affect digital transformation? The thesis investigates three channels using theory and prior evidence: funding restrictions, governance and monitoring, and knowledge spillovers. In empirical implementation, this thesis directly tests the first two channels and treats the knowledge channel as a deferred empirical extension requiring direct mediator variables.
The methodological objective is to combine proven NLP-based measurement with quasi-experimental econometrics in a transparent, reproducible, and appropriate manner for large-scale panel data. The key objective is to determine whether international financial integration may be used as a stimulus for technological advancement within firms.  The policy objective is to offer information to capital market regulators, institutional investors, and corporate executives about the larger technology implications of market-opening changes.
The thesis follows with five operational aims in order to attain these objectives. It generates high-coverage firm-year digital transformation metrics from annual reports. It employs baseline and dynamic DID techniques to assess therapy efficacy. It assesses inference stability by doing robustness and sensitivity tests. It uses heterogeneous effects to identify boundary conditions. It conducts mechanism evaluations to assess financing, governance, and knowledge channels. The research questions and objectives are intentionally integrated. The first question establishes whether a main effect exists. The second identifies where and for whom this effect is strongest. The third explains why the effect occurs. Together, they produce a coherent empirical narrative rather than disconnected statistical exercises.
1.3	The Empirical Setting: China’s A-Share Market and MSCI Index Inclusion
The empirical background for this argument is the gradual inclusion of Chinese A-shares in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index, a watershed moment in China's financial opening process Y. Liu et al. (2024). Prior to 2018, global investors had limited access to China's domestic equity market due to ownership restrictions, quota systems, and market-access frictions. Although conduits such as QFII, RQFII, and the Stock Connect programs existed, broad passive global allocation to mainland A-shares remained limited Awokuse et al. (2009).
MSCI inclusion changed this environment by connecting eligible A-share companies to benchmark-driven international capital flows Wei (2020). MSCI indexes are often used by global institutional investors and index-tracking funds. Being included in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index affects active portfolio building, analyst focus, and investor relations practices, in addition to passive investment flows P. Li et al. (2023). The reform therefore modified firms’ exposure to external financing and oversight conditions in a way that is economically significant.
Phases of the inclusion procedure developed, which is useful for identification. Phase I was put into effect in June 2018 with 234 large-cap A-shares at a 5% inclusion factor after the June 2017 announcement. By November 2019, Phase II increased coverage to 472 securities with a higher inclusion factor. Clear treatment time and observable pre- and post-policy windows have been established by this phased implementation. One major advantage of this system is that inclusion criteria were mostly rule-based and established externally. Eligibility was based on market capitalization, liquidity, and Stock Connect accessibility rather than on discretionary judgments of corporations' digital strategies Yang et al. (2025). While no real-world situation is completely random, this institutional setup increases the likelihood that inclusion reflects exogenous variation in international capital access relative to firms' digital transformation paths.
China is also an ideal context because firms operate under substantial heterogeneity in regional development, industry composition, ownership structures, and governance quality. This heterogeneity allows the thesis to test whether liberalization effects are uniform or conditional on absorptive capacity Dong et al. (2023). Prior studies indicate that effects are often stronger in specific contexts, such as non-Eastern regions in some financing-related analyses, and among non-state or non-SME firms in others. Embedding these moderation patterns within the MSCI setting helps move beyond average treatment effects toward a richer understanding of distributional outcomes.
The setting further supports mechanism analysis. Previous evidence shows that digital transformation is linked to stronger analyst coverage Chen et al. (2022) improved debt financing and performance Li et al. (2024), and lower cost of equity capital P. Li et al. (2023). These outcomes align with the idea that access to external capital and market monitoring influence firms’ transformation incentives and capabilities. The MSCI reform provides a structured context to test whether these relationships strengthen after liberalization. In sum, the Chinese A-share MSCI inclusion episode offers a rare combination of economic significance, policy clarity, and empirical tractability. It is both substantively important and methodologically suitable for understanding how international financial integration can affect firm-level digital transformation.
1.4	Overview of Research Design and Data
The empirical design combines causal identification with large-scale computational measurement. The main approach is a two-way fixed effects difference-in-differences model comparing firms that are included in MSCI (treated) and those that are not (untreated) before and after inclusion. This baseline is supplemented by event-study estimation to evaluate dynamic responses and pre-trend validity, propensity score matching DID to enhance comparability on observables, and placebo-based inference checks.
The data cover 35,264 firm-year observations from 2010 to 2022, representing 2,712 Chinese listed firms after sample cleaning. Core financial and governance variables are drawn from CSMAR, while treatment status is derived from MSCI constituent lists. Digital transformation outcomes are constructed from annual-report text using NLP-based keyword extraction across six technology domains: AI/machine learning, big data, cloud computing, IoT, blockchain/distributed ledger technologies, and enterprise digitization programs.
The baseline outcome is a composite index using log transformation of aggregated keyword frequencies. This approach addresses right skewness and enhances interpretability when disclosure dispersion is high. For robustness, alternative outcomes are also used, including domain-specific indices, normalized measures adjusted for report length, and additional transformations to assess sensitivity to scaling choices. Assessment of identification validity involves multiple strategies. Event-study coefficients verify that there is no significant divergence before treatment and examine whether effects persist after treatment. Placebo tests with randomized treatment assignments determine if observed effects are likely due to random chance. Alternative clustering methods and restricted-sample checks evaluate whether statistical significance relies on specific inference assumptions.
Mechanism analysis is embedded in the design rather than added as a descriptive task. Proxies related to financing constraints and governance quality are estimated through structured regressions to check whether channel patterns match the conceptual framework. The knowledge-spillover channel is retained at the theoretical level but not directly estimated in the baseline because direct mediator variables are not yet available in the merged panel. This step is crucial because the thesis's contribution depends not only on showing that effects exist but also on demonstrating logically consistent pathways. The design also acknowledges limitations of text-based outcomes. Annual reports may include strategic language and varying verbosity unrelated to actual investment. To address this, the study controls for report length, uses normalized keyword measures, and compares results across different specifications. The goal is not to claim perfect measurement of digital investment but to provide strong, consistent evidence across multiple measures.
Overall, the research design is deliberately triangulated: one treatment setting, multiple estimators, multiple outcome measures, and various mechanism tests. This triangulation enhances confidence that the results reflect genuine economic effects rather than model-specific artifacts.

2.1	 Capital Market Liberalization: Theory and Evidence 
2.1.1	Theoretical Foundations of Financial Liberalization
Capital market liberalization generally refers to removing barriers that separate domestic financial systems from international portfolio flows Long et al. (2024). In essence, liberalizing equity markets can improve allocative efficiency by enabling capital transfer to higher-productivity companies, lowering financing costs through more investor participation, and increasing risk-sharing via diversified ownership structures Zhu et al. (2023). These mechanisms are crucial in macro-finance and corporate finance models of integration. The traditional welfare argument suggests that firms facing financial constraints should benefit more when foreign capital is available Yin et al. (2023). In segmented markets, limitations of domestic savings, home bias, and informational frictions can sustain high costs linked to external finance. Liberalization efforts can reduce these frictions by boosting competition among investors and increasing market liquidity H. Liu et al. (2024). When firms have profitable projects with positive net present value (NPV) but lack sufficient funding, lowering financing costs can trigger actual investment growth.
Nevertheless, theoretical frameworks also highlight non-linearities. The benefits linked to liberalization depend on the quality of institutions, the effectiveness of legal enforcement, disclosure standards, and the strength of market infrastructure Stiglitz (2004). In environments with weak institutional frameworks, liberalization may increase market volatility or shift resources toward short-term opportunities instead of encouraging productive long-term investments J.-X. Zhang et al. (2023). This condition is especially relevant in emerging markets where regulatory development and corporate governance standards vary. From a corporate perspective, liberalization can be seen as a change in the optimization problems facing managers and controlling shareholders Cheng & Masron (2023). It affects the relative cost of external financing, the expected investment horizon of investors, and the level of monitoring for capital provision Chen et al. (2025). Projects with delayed but potentially significant returns, such as digital transformation initiatives within companies, may become more appealing under these modified conditions.
The MSCI inclusion mechanism is particularly informative because it combines signaling and flow effects. Inclusion can automatically trigger passive fund allocations while also signaling quality and accessibility to active investors. These dual effects can impact not only valuation and liquidity but also firms’ strategic planning, disclosure practices, and investment timing.
2.1.2	Effects on Corporate Investment, Innovation, and Governance
Empirical studies generally find that market opening influences corporate behavior through investment and governance channelsTian et al. (2023). On the investment side, reduced financing frictions can broaden firms’ feasible project optionsR. Zhang et al. (2023). Firms may increase R&D, capital expenditure, and strategic intangible investments when expected financing constraints lessenYin et al. (2023). On the governance side, foreign institutional investors can strengthen monitoring and raise transparency expectations. The governance channel is important because digital transformation involves difficult internal decisions: reallocating budgets, redesigning workflows, retraining employees, and accepting short-term performance pressuresR. Zhang et al. (2023). In environments with weak monitoring, managers may underinvest due to career-risk aversion or overinvest in symbolic initiatives with limited operational value. Improved external oversight can discipline these decisions.
Evidence from previous studies supports this logic.Chen & Zhang (2024) demonstrate that digital transformation correlates with increased analyst coverage and better information accuracy. This indicates a feedback loop: as firms digitize and communicate more effectively, external information environments improve, which can further reduce capital costs and encourage future investments.Du & Wang (2024) provide direct evidence that financial support enhances the innovation effects of digital transformation.
 Their findings show that the financial development context is important and that support from capital markets can be especially relevant in regions with more binding traditional constraints. This aligns with the idea that liberalization is most effective when it alleviates real financing bottlenecks rather than simply reallocates abundant capital. Chen & Zhang (2024; Feng et al. (2024) expand this perspective to corporate financing structures. They demonstrate that firms undergoing digital transformation achieve better debt financing results and lower equity costs, respectivelyXue & Zhang (2024). These findings suggest a potentially reinforcing mechanism: liberalization facilitates digital adoption, and successful digitalization then enhances financing terms, leading to increased investment capacity. 
2.1.3	Evidence from Emerging Market Contexts
Emerging markets provide valuable evidence because they combine high growth potential with institutional frictions that highlight financing channels. Studies in these markets often detect stronger real effects of financial reforms compared to more developed markets where constraints are already less severeStiglitz & Ocampo (2008). The Chinese evidence is especially useful due to detailed data and specific policy information. Research using MSCI inclusion and related reforms generally reports changes in liquidity, information processing, and firm behaviorYan (2023). However, a direct causal link from liberalization shocks to firm-level digital transformation outcomes is less developed. Most existing studies focus on innovation proxies, market quality measures, or financing conditions separately.
Previous studies show that contextual heterogeneity is important. Effects differ depending on regional market development, ownership structure, and firm size. For example, some analyses find stronger evidence for liberalization in non-Eastern regions, while others see more noticeable digital-response patterns in non-state firms or among different size groupsZhang et al. (2024). This heterogeneity indicates that average treatment estimates might hide significant distributional differences.
Another recurring insight in emerging-market research is that policy shocks interact with organizational absorptive capacityStiglitz (2000). Firms with better governance, managerial skills, or technology readiness may convert external financing opportunities into tangible digital investments more efficientlyR. Zhang et al. (2023). This interaction encourages heterogeneity analyses in later chapters of this thesis. Overall, the emerging-market literature strongly supports studying liberalization effects on digital transformation, but it has yet to develop a comprehensive, mechanism-based framework within a single quasi-experimental design. This thesis aims to fill that gap.
2.2	Digital Transformation: Conceptual Framework and Determinants
2.2.1	Defining Digital Transformation: Technological and Organizational Dimensions
Digital transformation extends beyond just acquiring technology. It involves continuous, firm-wide reconfiguration of value creation, operational processes, and organizational coordination through digital technologiesYang (2023). A narrow view that only considers software or hardware spending misses the strategic and organizational aspects that influence whether digital investments lead to productivity improvements. Conceptually, digital transformation includes at least three layersVerhoef et al. (2021; Xue & Zhang (2024). The first is infrastructure adoption, which covers data architecture, cloud resources, and connected systems. The second is process transformation, where digital tools change production, logistics, customer interactions, and managerial decision-makingBerman (2012). The third is organizational transformation, encompassing governance redesign, capability development, and strategic adaptation.
These layers depend on each other. Infrastructure without process integration can yield limited resultsWang & Li (2024). Process changes without organizational incentives can prevent progress. Organizational commitment without funding can remain superficial. Therefore, digital transformation is best viewed as a coordinated investment journey rather than a single technological eventXue & Zhang (2024). For empirical research, this multidimensional concept presents measurement challenges. Researchers need indicators that are broad enough to capture cross-functional transformation but specific enough to differentiate digital activity from general corporate rhetoricChanias et al. (2019). The text-based method used in this thesis aims to balance this by combining domain-specific keyword structures with validation and robustness checks.
2.2.2	Core Technology Domains: AI, Big Data, Cloud Computing, IoT, and Blockchain
The technology areas highlighted in this thesis align with key trends in modern corporate digitalization. Artificial intelligence and machine learning support prediction, optimization, and automation across both front-end and back-end operationsBabina et al. (2024). Big data systems enable companies to turn large, fast-moving information into operational insights. Cloud computing offers scalable infrastructure that reduces fixed ICT costs and allows flexible deploymentAdeola & Evans (2020). Internet of Things technologies link physical assets to digital monitoring and control systems, enhancing real-time visibility and enabling predictive maintenanceKommisetty & Dileep (2022). Blockchain and distributed ledger technology provide traceability, tamper resistance, and coordination across organizational boundariesAnsari et al. (2025). Enterprise digitization initiatives combine these technologies with ERP upgrades, process engineering, and platform strategies.
These domains vary in capital intensity, knowledge requirements, and implementation risks. Cloud migration may require significant infrastructure costs and vendor coordination. AI deployment requires high-quality data, specialized talent, and iterative model governance. IoT systems often need hardware-software integration in production settingsAnsari et al. (2025). Due to these differences, external financing and access to knowledge can have varied effects across domainsWang et al. (2024). Existing research supports this multidimensional view. Studies show broad connections between digital transformation, information quality, financing results, and innovation performanceRen et al. (2024). These findings suggest that digital transformation should be seen as a spectrum of intensities across multiple technological and organizational channels.
2.2.3	Drivers, Barriers, and Firm-Level Heterogeneity
Drivers of digital transformation include financial slack, managerial capability, data infrastructure, and complementary human capital. Firms with stronger internal coordination and strategic alignment are more likely to pursue coherent transformation paths rather than isolated digital projects. Leadership commitment and governance design are especially important because digital initiatives often cross departmental boundaries and need ongoing execution. Barriers are also significant. High uncertainty about benefits can discourage investment, particularly in firms under pressure for short-term performance. Talent shortages in data engineering, AI deployment, and cybersecurity limit progressZhang & Cao (2023). Legacy systems can increase integration costs and elevate implementation risk. Regulatory uncertainty around data governance and privacy can further delay adoption.
These barriers imply heterogeneous response capacities. Larger firms may benefit from economies of scale in technology investment and internal skill developmentGao et al. (2024). Companies in developed regions might access more extensive supplier networks, digital infrastructure, and a skilled labor force. Non-state firms often face greater market competition, creating stronger incentives to adopt productivity-enhancing digital strategies. State-linked firms may have better access to financing but might face less pressure for rapid organizational change. The overall outcome remains an empirical questionJin & Pan (2023).
Observed heterogeneity patterns in the literature support this reasoning. Several studies report different effects based on market development level, ownership structure, and firm size. This evidence encourages treatment-effect decomposition in the empirical chapters instead of relying solely on pooled averages.
2.2.4	Digital Transformation in Emerging Markets: Opportunities and Constraints
Emerging markets offer a unique digitalization landscape with both growth opportunities and structural challenges. While firms can bypass traditional stages by adopting modern cloud-native systems, mobile-first strategies, and data-driven operations, there are significant differences in institutional capacity, access to financing, and digital infrastructure across regions and sectorsZhao et al. (2023). In these situations, access to external capital markets can serve a catalytic function. International investors may not only supply funding but also bring governance oversight, benchmarking standards, and a focus on long-term capability building. These elements are especially important when domestic financing options are short-term focused or risk-averse toward intangible investmentsLi (2025).
Simultaneously, integration into global capital markets can subject firms to short-term performance scrutiny and disclosure expectations. Without strong governance and organizational capabilities, firms might resort to symbolic digital narratives instead of meaningful transformationYu (2022). This highlights the need for measurement and robustness checks that differentiate between disclosure levels and actual investment effects. Specifically for China, the coexistence of advanced digital ecosystems and uneven regional development creates a particularly revealing contextDu et al. (2024). Some firms operate close to global digital frontiers, while others encounter capability and infrastructure gaps. Capital market liberalization may thus lead to both acceleration and divergence, emphasizing the importance of analyzing heterogeneity. 
2.3	Mechanisms Linking Capital Market Liberalization to Digital Transformation
2.3.1	The Financing Constraint Channel
The financing-constraint mechanism is the most direct way connecting liberalization to digital transformation. Digital projects often require large upfront expenses in software systems, data architecture, cloud migration, machine learning platforms, and organizational training. Many of these investments yield returns only after a delay and with some uncertainty, making them hard to finance through traditional credit systemsKose et al. (2003). By broadening access to equity investors and lowering the returns they expect, liberalization can reduce the user cost of capital for such projects. Being included in global benchmarks may also boost liquidity and price discovery, which can ease financing difficulties in both equity and debt markets. If firms see improved financing conditions as lasting rather than temporary, they are more likely to pursue multi-year digital programsLi & Pang (2023).
Existing studies strongly support this channel. Hordofa (2024)show that capital-market financial support significantly enhances the innovation effects of digital transformation, with significance at the 1% level Yan (2023)report that digital transformation is linked to stronger debt financing and better performance. Y. Liu et al. (2024)find lower cost of equity among firms undergoing digital transformation. These results suggest that financing and digitalization can reinforce each other. The channel is unlikely to be consistent across all firms. Firms with initially binding financing constraints should show larger responses. Regional financial development, firm size, and ownership structure can all influence the effect's magnitudeHuang et al. (2023). This aligns with evidence that effects differ by market development and non-Eastern regional contexts in the compiled studies.
2.3.2	Corporate Governance and International Investor Monitoring 
A second mechanism is governance upgrading through international investor monitoring. Foreign institutional investors often demand higher disclosure quality, stronger board accountability, and clearer strategic communication. Under these conditions, managers may have stronger incentives to focus on long-term value-enhancing projects and reduce agency-driven underinvestmentChen et al. (2025). Digital transformation is sensitive to governance. It requires coordination across departments, long-term resource commitments, and tolerance for transitional performance fluctuations. Weak governance can result in fragmented implementation, opportunistic disclosure, or premature project termination. Improved monitoring can enhance project selection, enforce disciplined execution, and improve post-investment evaluation.
Ma et al. (2025) provide relevant supporting evidence by showing that digital transformation is linked to improved information environments through increased analyst coverage and better information accuracy. This finding aligns with stronger transparency dynamics and more credible communication between firms and capital providers. Governance effects may also interact with ownership structureC. Li et al. (2023). In firms with concentrated ownership or weaker protections for minority shareholders, external monitoring from global investors can add extra discipline. In other firms, governance structures might already be strong, lessening their marginal impact. These empirical possibilities encourage explicit channel and heterogeneity tests rather than one-size-fits-all assumptions.
2.3.3	Knowledge Spillovers and Global Best Practice Diffusion
A third mechanism operates through information and knowledge spillovers. Capital market liberalization boosts interactions between domestic firms and global investors, analysts, and intermediaries who often have extensive experience with digital strategies across various industries and countriesZheng et al. (2021). Through investor meetings, analyst reports, benchmarking exercises, and governance engagement, firms can access implementation templates and performance standards. Knowledge spillovers are especially important when digital projects need tacit managerial skills, not just financial resourcesSun et al. (2022; Zhu et al. (2023). Firms might require guidance on project sequencing, vendor selection, data governance, cybersecurity, and organizational change management. Access to global networks can lower trial-and-error costs and speed up learning curves.
This mechanism aligns with broader themes in the literature, which highlight market development effects, regional differences, and ownership-sensitive outcomes. These patterns suggest that the value of external knowledge depends on local absorptive capacityH. Liu et al. (2024). Firms with stronger managerial skills or ecosystem support can better transform external signals into operational changesLi & Shen (2021). The knowledge channel also works alongside financing and governance channels. Better knowledge can enhance project quality, making financing more appealing and governance oversight more effective. In this way, these mechanisms are interconnected rather than isolated.
For empirical implementation, the current finance literature generally treats proxy quality as hierarchical rather than equivalent. Direct transmission proxies are strongest: analyst coverage (and optionally forecast dispersion) and foreign institutional ownership shares, because both map closely to the mechanism chain MSCI → external information intermediaries/investors → firm learning and strategic adjustment. A second tier includes cross-border linkage measures (export intensity, overseas subsidiaries, foreign directors), which capture exposure to global knowledge networks but with more heterogeneous interpretation.
2.3.4	Market Signaling, Certification, and Reputational Effects
Liberalization can impact digital transformation through signaling and certification processes. Inclusion in a major global benchmark is often seen as recognition of minimum standards in accessibility, liquidity, and market relevanceC. Li et al. (2023). This signal can lower external uncertainty about a firm's quality and boost confidence among investors, customers, technology partners, and skilled labor. For firms, this certification may enhance strategic options. Improved market visibility can help establish partnerships with digital vendors, attract specialized talent, and boost credibility in supply-chain digital integration. Reputational improvements can also support internal organizational momentum by aligning managerial incentives with publicly visible transformation goals.
Signaling can produce both substantive and symbolic effects. Substantive effects occur when improved reputation reduces transaction frictions and opens up strategic opportunities. Symbolic effects happen when firms enhance digital rhetoric without making corresponding operational investmentsDong et al. (2023). Distinguishing between these outcomes is an empirical challenge that is addressed using robustness designs, including normalized text measures and mechanism consistency checks. In summary, signaling is not a standalone mechanism but an amplifier that interacts with financing, governance, and knowledge channels. Its overall impact depends on whether firms convert reputational gains into real capability-building investments.
2.4	Text-Based Measurement of Firm Characteristics 
2.4.1	Computational Approaches in Corporate Finance and Economics
The rise of text-as-data methods has revolutionized empirical corporate research by enabling high-frequency, large-sample analysis of otherwise hidden constructs. Corporate disclosures, earnings reports, and regulatory filings now offer rich textual signals about strategy, risk perception, governance priorities, and innovation focusYang et al. (2024). In the era of digital transformation, computational text methods are especially valuable because direct accounting measures are often incomplete. Many transformation efforts are embedded in operational expenses, intangible reorganizations, and process redesigns that are not separately reported in financial statements. Text disclosures provide additional insight into strategic direction and implementation focusBellstam et al. (2017).
Methodologically, text-based approaches enhance scalability and consistency compared to purely manual coding. They enable researchers to build firm-year panels over long durations and across diverse industriesAhci & Joos (2023). This is especially useful in emerging markets where survey data may be limited or unreliable. However, computational methods need careful validation to prevent mechanical or semantic mistakes. Keyword design, language segmentation, and context disambiguation can influence inference accuracyZimmermann et al. (2023). Therefore, the methodology in this thesis focuses on transparent dictionary building, domain-specific customization, and multiple validation checks.
2.4.2	NLP-Based Measurement of Innovation and Digital Activity
Recent studies have demonstrated that Chinese-language annual reports can provide credible NLP-based indicators of digital activity. The data collected in this project shows strong correlations between text-derived digital indices and observable technology-related outcomes such as IT investment and digital patentingAfzal (2024). These correlations support construct validity, although they recognize that no single indicator fully measures the depth of transformation. This method relies on domain-cluster keyword extraction instead of counting generic buzzwords, which is an important distinctionKumar (2024). A well-designed dictionary should accurately capture meaningful technological and organizational content across AI, big data, cloud, IoT, blockchain, and enterprise digitization groups. Domain clustering enhances interpretability and allows for heterogeneity analysis based on technology type.
NLP measurement is not meant to substitute financial indicators, patent data, or case evidence. Instead, it enhances them by providing broad-coverage strategic signals. In empirical designs with causal treatment variation, text-derived outcomes can indicate whether policy shocks are linked to systematic changes in firms’ digital orientation and reported implementation activitiesY. Li et al. (2023). The literature further supports this approach by demonstrating connections between digital transformation and financing variables, information quality, and performance outcomes. This external links boost confidence that digital disclosure measures reflect economically meaningful firm behavior.
2.4.3	Validation Strategies for Text-Derived Indices
Validation is central to any text-based empirical strategy. This thesis applies three validation layers: content validity, construct validity, and robustness validity.
•	Content validity is established through careful dictionary design and expert review. Terms are selected to represent specific technological and organizational facets of digital transformation rather than broad innovation language. Ambiguous terms are filtered or checked in context to minimize false positives.
•	Construct validity is evaluated by comparing text-derived indices with related observable variables when available, such as IT-oriented spending proxies or digital patent activity. Positive and meaningful correlations support the idea that text indicators reflect real variation in digital engagement.
•	The validity of robustness is evaluated through various alternative outcome structures, including normalization by report length, transformed and untransformed measures, and technology-specific sub-indices. Consistent treatment patterns across these alternatives lessen concerns that the primary findings are artifacts of a single specification.
An additional practical validity check is the coherence of the mechanism. If digital-outcome effects are real, they should be reflected in plausible changes in financing, governance, and knowledge proxies. If the effects are purely rhetorical, evidence from these channels should be weak or inconsistent. The thesis uses this logic to supplement statistical validation.
2.4.4	Challenges in Chinese-Language Disclosure Environments
Chinese-language textual analysis presents technical and interpretive challenges. Chinese text segmentation is complex because characters are written without spaces. Domain-specific terms often combine technical and managerial meanings, and some words can change meaning depending on contextY. Li et al. (2023; Wang et al. (2022). Therefore, accurate segmentation and disambiguation are crucial. Another challenge is variation in strategic disclosure. Firms differ in annual report style, verbosity, and communication strategyYang et al. (2024). Larger or more visible firms may produce longer, more detailed disclosures regardless of their actual digital intensity. Without correction, keyword counts can simply reflect document length.
This thesis addresses such concerns through several measures: domain-specific dictionary refinement, stop-word filtering, alternative normalization based on report length, and regression controls for disclosure scale. Sensitivity analyses across different specifications help determine whether estimated treatment effects remain consistent under stricter measurement conditions. A third challenge involves distinguishing between intention and implementation. Disclosures may report plans, pilot projects, or strategic narratives that do not immediately lead to operational outcomes. For this reason, the thesis interprets text-based digital indices as measures of transformation intensity in disclosed strategic and operational focus, while also supporting this interpretation with evidence on mechanisms and heterogeneity.
Despite these challenges, Chinese annual reports remain some of the most comprehensive and standardized data sources for long-term firm-level analysis. With careful design and validation, they offer a strong empirical foundation for studying digital transformation at scale.
2.5	Research Gaps and Positioning of This Study
The reviewed literature reveals four core gaps that motivate this thesis.
•	The first gap is a linkage gap. Research on capital market liberalization and research on digital transformation have mostly developed independently. Liberalization studies focus on financing and market outcomes, while digital studies emphasize organizational and technological drivers. There is limited causal evidence directly linking liberalization shocks to digital transformation intensity.
•	The second gap is an identification gap. Many studies in related fields rely on cross-sectional correlations or panel models that may face endogeneity issues. Firms with stronger hidden digital capabilities might also secure better financing conditions, making it hard to determine the true causal direction. A quasi-experimental design with a clear treatment timeline is necessary to improve the credibility of identification.
•	The third gap is a mechanism integration gap. Existing evidence suggests financing, governance, and knowledge channels are all plausible, but these channels are often examined in isolation. A unified framework that tests all three pathways under a common treatment setting is still limited.
•	The fourth gap is a measurement scalability gap. Direct digital investment metrics are often incomplete in emerging-market accounting systems, while survey methods offer limited panel depth. Scalable, validated text-based measures are necessary to analyze long-term, large-sample digital dynamics with consistent methodology.
This thesis addresses these gaps by examining the MSCI A-share inclusion event as a near-natural experiment to determine the causal effect of liberalization on digital transformation. It uses a large firm-year panel with NLP-based digital measurement and verifies findings across baseline, dynamic, robustness, heterogeneity, and mechanism analyses. The literature synthesis provides direct support for this positioning. The selected studies consistently show that digital transformation is linked to improved information environments, financing conditions, innovation outcomes, and firm performance. They also reveal context dependence by region, ownership, and firm scale. What is missing is a design that integrates these insights into a single coherent causal framework. This thesis is constructed to provide that framework.
In contribution terms, the study does not attempt to replace existing findings but to connect them. It links financial liberalization to digital transformation, links average effects to heterogeneous effects, and links outcome effects to channel evidence. This integrated approach is the central value added of the literature review and the broader thesis design.

3.1	China's Capital Market Structure Prior to Liberalization
3.1.1	The A-Share Market and Domestic Investor Segmentation
China's A-share market—the renminbi-denominated equity market listed on the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges—was historically subject to strict foreign ownership restrictions and capital controls, which created near-complete segmentation from international capital flows. Cheng et al. (2024) Before liberalization reforms, foreign investors could access mainland Chinese equities only through the limited QFII (Qualified Foreign Institutional Investor) and RQFII programs, which imposed strict quotas and administrative barriers. This segmentation meant that Chinese listed firms relied almost entirely on domestic capital markets, characterized by shorter investment horizons, weaker corporate governance standards, and limited exposure to global best practices.
3.1.2	Stock Connect Programs as the Technical Infrastructure for Liberalization 
The technical infrastructure for capital market liberalization was established through the Stock Connect programs: Shanghai-Hong Kong Stock Connect (launched November 2014) and Shenzhen-Hong Kong Stock Connect (launched December 2016)Wang & Chia (2014). These programs developed daily and aggregate quotas, currency hedging methods, and settlement protocols that approved foreign investors to buy A-shares while adhering to capital control regimes (Parviainen et al.2022). By 2018, these systems were sufficiently developed to handle the substantial capital movements associated with MSCI inclusionYin et al. (2023).
3.2	The MSCI Emerging Markets Index and the A-Share Inclusion Process
3.2.1	MSCI as a Global Benchmark: Scale, Influence, and Index Composition
The MSCI Emerging Markets Index is the main benchmark for global institutional investors investing in developing market equities, with about $1.9 trillion in assets under management tracking or comparing against MSCI emerging market indices in 2018. The index's composition determines mandatory portfolio allocations for passive funds and acts as a reference for active managers, influencing capital flows to the included companiesWei (2020). 
3.2.2	Inclusion Criteria: Market Capitalization, Liquidity, and Stock Connect Accessibility
MSCI's inclusion criteria were mechanical rather than discretionary: (i) a minimum free-float adjusted market capitalization threshold; (ii) minimum liquidity requirements based on annualized trading volume and turnover ratios; and (iii) accessibility through Stock Connect programs. Importantly, these criteria are independent of firms' underlying digitalization paths or performance, offering a plausible source of exogenous variation in international capital access.
3.2.3	Pre-Announcement and Phased Implementation Timeline
MSCI announced the inclusion decision on June 20, 2017 — a full year before the first implementation tranche — providing firms with time to prepare for international investor engagement. The announcement itself constitutes a clean event that allows testing of announcement effects separately from implementation effectsHu et al. (2018).
3.2.4	Phase I (June 2018): 234 Large-Cap A-Shares at 5% Inclusion Factor
The initial tranche, launched on June 1, 2018, incorporated 234 large-cap A-shares at a 5% inclusion factor. This accounted for about 0.73% of the total MSCI Emerging Markets Index. The modest size aimed to evaluate market preparedness and reduce impact, while allowing the included companies to experience international passive fund inflows for the first time.
3.2.5	Phase II (November 2019): Expansion to 472 Securities at 20% Factor
By November 2019, the index grew to include 472 A-shares across large, mid, and small-cap categories, with a 20% inclusion rate. This significantly boosted the presence of Chinese stocks in the index and broadened the scope to include smaller firms, expanding our universe of treated firms. 
3.2.6	Quasi-Experimental Properties of the Inclusion Design
Three features make MSCI inclusion an almost perfect quasi-experiment. First, the selection criteria are mechanical and unrelated to digitalization propensity. Second, the timeline was externally imposed by MSCI, independent of Chinese government or firm decisions. Third, the phased implementation establishes clear pre- and post-treatment periods and multiple cohorts, enabling staggered adoption analysis.
3.3	Hypothesis Development
Building on the theoretical considerations and institutional background outlined above, we develop three testable hypotheses.
3.3.1	H1: Main Effect — Capital Market Liberalization and Digital Transformation Intensity 
Hypothesis 1 (H1 — Main Effect): Capital market liberalization, as indicated by MSCI inclusion, is positively linked to the intensity of corporate digital transformation. Firms included in MSCI gain access to international capital, improved governance mechanisms, and global knowledge networks — all of which reduce the costs and increase the benefits of digital transformation investments.
3.3.2	H2: Technology Heterogeneity — Capital- and Knowledge-Intensive Domains
Hypothesis 2 (H2 — Technology Heterogeneity): The effects of capital market liberalization vary across digital technology types, with stronger effects for capital-intensive technologies (cloud computing, big data infrastructure) and knowledge-intensive technologies (artificial intelligence, advanced analytics) that particularly benefit from international financing and knowledge spillovers.
3.3.3	H3: Regional and Firm Heterogeneity — Absorptive Capacity and Institutional Quality
Hypothesis 3 (H3 — Regional and Firm Heterogeneity): Effects are moderated by regional development levels and firm-specific characteristics, with stronger treatment responses for firms in more developed regions with better institutional quality, larger firms with greater absorptive capacity, and firms in technology-intensive industries.

 
Chapter 4	Research Design I: Causal Identification of Capital Market Liberalization Effects
This chapter presents the causal identification strategy used to estimate the effect of capital market liberalization on firm-level digital transformation. It clarifies treatment construction, sample design, model specification, and identification assumptions in a transparent sequence. Consistent with state-of-the-art empirical standards in applied economics and corporate finance, the chapter combines baseline DID estimation with dynamic tests, matching-based diagnostics, and explicit discussion of possible threats to internal validity.
The chapter is intentionally organized as a credibility protocol. Each design component is introduced together with the specific inferential risk it is meant to address, including selection on observables, differential pre-trends, timing heterogeneity, and disclosure-measurement artifacts. This structure allows readers to evaluate not only the final estimator but also the chain of supporting assumptions and diagnostics that justify interpretation. In this sense, Chapter 4 functions as the methodological backbone of the thesis.
4.1	Sample and Data Source
This section documents the empirical foundation of the analysis: data origin, sample filters, treatment assignment rules, and final panel structure. The focus is reproducibility and comparability, ensuring that each analytical step can be replicated from primary databases and official index records.
4.1.1	CSMAR Financial and Corporate Governance Database
Financial statements, market characteristics, and corporate governance information are derived from the China Stock Market and Accounting Research (CSMAR) database, maintained by the Shenzhen GTA Information Technology Co. CSMAR covers all Chinese A-share firms listed on the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges and provides standardized panel data on financial, governance, and text analysis variables. Its primary advantage for this study is internal consistency across modules, which allows firm identifiers, accounting variables, governance indicators, and text-derived measures to be linked without ad hoc reconciliation. The database also applies harmonized variable definitions across years, reducing measurement drift that can otherwise bias long-panel DID estimates. These properties are particularly important in a setting where treatment timing is externally determined and causal interpretation relies on clean within-firm comparisons over time.
4.1.2	MSCI Emerging Markets Index Constituent Lists
MSCI constituent lists and weight schedules are compiled from official MSCI Emerging Markets Index releases published at www.msci.com. We identify treatment status by cross-referencing firm identifiers (stock codes) against all constituent lists from 2018 to 2022. A firm is defined as treated from the year of its first inclusion onwardDescy (). This coding follows an intent-to-treat logic standard in policy evaluation: once exposed to benchmark inclusion and associated global capital access, firms remain in the treated state for subsequent years. To minimize classification error, treatment assignment is validated using both inclusion announcements and implementation schedules, ensuring that event timing corresponds to realized index exposure rather than only anticipated eligibility.
4.1.3	Sample Period: 2010–2022
The sample spans 2010 to 2022, providing four pre-treatment years at baseline (2010–2017) and up to four post-treatment years (2018–2022). The pre-treatment window is sufficient to validate parallel trends assumptions; the post-treatment window captures both immediate and sustained effects. From a design perspective, this horizon balances two competing goals: obtaining enough pre-period observations for credible trend diagnostics while retaining a long enough post-period to observe dynamic accumulation in digital transformation outcomes. The window also covers major macro and regulatory variation, allowing year fixed effects and robustness checks to absorb common shocks that might otherwise confound treatment estimates.
4.1.4	Exclusion Criteria: Financial Institutions and Regulated Utilities
We exclude financial institutions (banking, insurance, securities, and trust companies), heavily regulated utilities, and firms with incomplete annual reports during the sample period. Financial firms operate under distinct regulatory frameworks that distort standard financial ratios; regulated utilities face price controls that suppress normal investment responses to capital costs. Firms with materially incomplete disclosure are excluded because the dependent variable is text-based and requires stable reporting coverage for consistent inference. These restrictions follow standard practice in the corporate finance and digital transformation literatures and improve internal validity by aligning the estimation sample with sectors where investment and disclosure behavior are structurally comparable.
4.1.5	Treatment Assignment: MSCI Constituent Identification
Firms are assigned to treatment (MSCI = 1) if they appear on any MSCI Emerging Markets Index constituent list between 2018 and 2022. Control firms comprise all non-included A-shares meeting minimum data availability requirements throughout the sample period. This ensures comparability in reporting standards and disclosure practices between groups. Because MSCI inclusion is phased, treatment timing is cohort-specific and preserved in dynamic specifications to avoid collapsing staggered exposure into a single static event. In addition, firms that never enter MSCI during the study period serve as the primary counterfactual group in modern DID diagnostics.
4.1.6	Final Sample: 35,264 Firm-Year Observations, 2,712 Unique Firms
The final dataset contains 35,264 firm-year observations representing 2,712 unique firms, with 1,644 observations corresponding to MSCI constituent (treated) firms. The ratio of treated-to-control observations (1,644 : 33,620) reflects MSCI's selective criteria targeting large-cap, liquid, and accessible firms. Although treatment prevalence is relatively low, this structure is typical for index-inclusion natural experiments and remains suitable for inference when fixed effects and clustered standard errors are applied. The large untreated pool also improves the precision of counterfactual trend estimation in event-study and placebo exercises.
4.2	Model Design
This section develops the econometric framework used to identify causal effects. It introduces the two-way fixed effects DID baseline, event-study dynamics, and matched-sample extensions, and explains how each specification addresses a distinct identification risk. The objective is not only to estimate an average treatment effect, but also to test whether that effect is temporally coherent, robust across specifications, and consistent with quasi-experimental identification logic.
Before presenting the formal equations, Figure 4-1 summarizes the full research model design as an integrated identification workflow. The figure links institutional treatment construction, sample and variable architecture, and outcome measurement to the three complementary estimation strategies used in this thesis (TWFE-DID, event study, and PSM-DID).

As a research model design, Figure 4-1 highlights the inferential logic of the chapter: causal identification does not rely on a single estimator, but on convergence across model structure, dynamic diagnostics, and robustness checks. 4.2.1	The Natural Experiment: Exogeneity and Identification Logic
MSCI inclusion meets the key requirements of a natural experiment. Inclusion criteria are mechanical — free-float market capitalization, liquidity thresholds, and Stock Connect accessibility — and are independent of firms' underlying digitalization trajectories. The policy schedule was enforced externally by MSCI, without input from Chinese authorities or individual firms. These properties generate plausibly exogenous variation in international capital access, supporting the causal interpretation of our DID estimates. Following contemporary causal-design standards, we do not rely on exogeneity by assertion alone; instead, we combine institutional argumentation with empirical diagnostics, including pre-trend checks, placebo assignments, and alternative estimators designed for staggered treatment settings.
4.2.2	Two-Way Fixed Effects Difference-in-Differences Specification
We estimate the following two-way fixed effects DID specification:

DTᵢₜ = α + β (MSCIᵢ × Postₜ) + γ′Xᵢₜ + μᵢ + λₜ + εᵢₜ 				 (Eq. 4-1)

where DTᵢₜ represents firm i's digital transformation score in fiscal year t; MSCIᵢ equals one for constituent firms; Postₜ equals one from 2018 onward; Xᵢₜ is a vector of time-varying control variables; μᵢ and λₜ capture firm and year fixed effects, respectively; and εᵢₜ is the idiosyncratic disturbance clustered at the firm level. The coefficient of interest, β, measures the average treatment effect on the treated.
4.2.3	Event Study Design and Dynamic Treatment Indicators
To analyze the temporal pattern of treatment effects and validate pre-treatment parallel trends, we estimate the event-study specification:

DTᵢₜ = α + Σₖ δₖ MSCIᵢ × 1[t − Tᵢ = k] + γ′Xᵢₜ + μᵢ + λₜ + εᵢₜ  		(Eq. 4-2)

where Tᵢ denotes firm i's inclusion year, and k = −1 is omitted as the reference period. Dynamic coefficients δₖ reveal whether digitalization accelerates precisely around the inclusion window and whether effects persist over time.
4.2.4	Propensity Score Matching Difference-in-Differences (PSM-DID)
To address residual selection on observable characteristics, we augment the baseline DID with propensity score matching. We estimate inclusion probability using pre-treatment (2016) characteristics via logistic regression, implement nearest-neighbor matching with replacement and a 0.01 caliper, and retain up to three controls per treated firm. Covariate balance diagnostics confirm standardized mean differences below 0.1 for all matching variables. Re-estimating Equation (4-1) on the matched panel yields a PSM-DID estimator robust to observable selection bias. Methodologically, the matched design is used as a complement rather than a substitute for fixed-effects identification: matching improves overlap on observables, while DID continues to absorb time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity and common shocks.
4.2.5	Threats to Identification and Mitigation Strategies
The primary threats to identification are: (i) pre-existing trends — firms selected into MSCI may have had faster-growing digitalization prior to inclusion; (ii) confounding events — contemporaneous reforms (e.g., STAR Market establishment, COVID-19) may differentially affect treated and control firms; and (iii) disclosure strategic response — firms may increase digital-related language post-inclusion without substantive operational changes"The World After COVID-19: Exploring the Market’s Digital Makeover - MSCI"). We address these through event study pre-trend tests, exclusion of contemporaneous reform events, disclosure-length robustness checks, and 1,000-iteration placebo tests. In addition, we report staggered-adoption diagnostics motivated by the modern DID literature, which cautions that conventional TWFE coefficients can be misleading under heterogeneous treatment effects. Taken together, these safeguards reduce the likelihood that results are driven by differential trends, timing artifacts, or pure disclosure inflation.

Chapter 5	Research Design II: Computational Text Analysis of Digital Transformation
This chapter develops the computational measurement architecture used to quantify digital transformation intensity from annual-report disclosures. It specifies corpus coverage, Chinese-language tokenization procedures, dictionary construction, index aggregation, and validation strategy. Following current best practice in text-as-data research, measurement is treated as a core inferential component rather than a purely technical preprocessing step.
The chapter further emphasizes that measurement error is a first-order concern in causal work using textual outcomes. It therefore documents not only how the index is built but also why specific preprocessing and validation choices improve construct validity in a Chinese-language disclosure environment. By combining linguistic adaptation, domain-specific dictionaries, and multiple robustness specifications, the chapter aims to show that estimated policy effects are not artifacts of one coding rule but stable patterns across plausible measurement alternatives.
5.1	Motivation: Why Text-Based Measurement for Digital Transformation?
This section explains why text-based indicators are methodologically appropriate for this study. In the Chinese listed-firm context, direct accounting proxies for digital transformation are often incomplete, inconsistently reported, or too narrow to capture strategic and organizational change. Annual-report narratives provide broad firm-year coverage and allow multidimensional measurement, while subsequent robustness tests help separate substantive transformation signals from disclosure-style noise.
5.1.1	Limitations of Traditional Proxies: IT Expenditure and Patent Counts
Traditional proxies for digital transformation face three limitations. IT expenditure data are not separately disclosed in Chinese annual reports, are unavailable at firm-year resolution for listed companies, and conflate maintenance spending with transformation investment. Patent counts capture only legally protectable innovations, excluding the organizational and process-level changes central to digital transformation; they are also subject to strategic patenting behavior that correlates weakly with actual operational change. More broadly, both indicators have limited coverage for service-sector digitalization, workflow redesign, and platform integration, which are central to real transformation but rarely captured in formal IP outputs. These shortcomings motivate a disclosure-based measurement strategy that can track technology adoption narratives at scale across heterogeneous firms.
5.1.2	The Case for Computational Analysis of Annual Report Disclosures
Annual reports are mandatory, standardized, and publicly available disclosures for all listed firms, providing comprehensive coverage of our sample without attrition. Prior validation studies Chen & Zhang (2024; Y. Li et al. (2023) confirm that keyword frequencies from Chinese annual reports positively correlate with IT investment (ρ = 0.67), software capitalization, and digital patent applications (ρ = 0.71). The approach enables analysis at scales previously infeasible in emerging markets where traditional survey data are scarce. From a methodological standpoint, computational text analysis also enables transparent preprocessing pipelines, reproducible dictionaries, and sensitivity checks across alternative index constructions, all of which are now expected in high-quality empirical text-as-data research.
5.2	Annual Report Corpus and Document Processing
5.2.1	Data Source: CSMAR Text Analysis Module
Digital transformation measures are obtained from the CSMAR Text Analysis module, which applies NLP pipelines to the full text of annual reports for all Chinese A-share listed firms. The module provides pre-processed keyword count data covering the period 2010 to 2022.
5.2.2	Scope and Coverage of the Annual Report Corpus
The dataset includes mandatory annual reports (年度报告, niándù bàogào) for all Shanghai and Shenzhen A-share firms, providing approximately 2,712 × 13 = 35,264 firm-year documents after applying our sample restrictions. Reports are obtained from the official CNINFO (www.cninfo.com.cn) disclosure platform.
5.2.3	Document Pre-Processing and Narrative Section Extraction
Processing extracts narrative sections — Management Discussion and Analysis, Business Overview, and Technology Strategy sections — while excluding financial statement tables, boilerplate legalese, and auditor reports that contain high frequencies of numerical characters relative to substantive text.
5.3	Chinese Text Tokenization and Segmentation
5.3.1	Jieba Segmentation Algorithm for Chinese-Language Text
Unlike English text, where words are separated by whitespace, Chinese text needs active segmentation because characters are written continuously. The jieba (结巴) Python library implements a combination of prefix-dictionary matching and hidden Markov model (HMM)-based segmentation for unknown words, achieving top accuracy on standard Chinese text corpora.
5.3.2	Stop Word Filtering and Domain-Specific Adaptations
Standard Chinese stop words (function words, particles, conjunctions) are removed before keyword matching. Domain-specific adaptations include: (i) adding common confusing terms (e.g., "数字" meaning "number" versus "digital") to disambiguation lists; (ii) developing a domain-specific financial vocabulary to prioritize tokenization; and (iii) using minimum keyword length filters (≥ 2 characters) to remove common single-character function words that match entries in the keyword dictionary.

5.5	Index Construction and Aggregation
5.5.1	Technology-Specific Sub-Indices
For each firm-year, we compute six technology-specific sub-indices DT_j = count of keywords in cluster j identified in the annual report text. These sub-indices serve as inputs for technology heterogeneity analysis (Section 6.3) and allow testing of Hypothesis 2. 
5.5.2	Composite Index: DT_Comp = log(1 + Σ keywords)
The primary outcome variable is the composite index DT_Comp = log(1 + Σⱼ DT_j), which aggregates keyword frequencies across all six clusters using a logarithmic transformation. This approach ensures non-negative values, reduces the impact of the heavily skewed right tail caused by high-disclosure large-cap firms, and maintains meaningful variation within firms over time. 
5.5.3	Logarithmic Transformation and Treatment of Extreme Values
In addition to winsorization (1st–99th percentile), the logarithmic transformation 1 + Σ DT_j reduces the mechanical influence of verbosity differences across firms. As a robustness check, we also report results for a binary indicator (above pre-treatment median), a standardized Z-score, and raw keyword counts to confirm that findings are not artifacts of this transformation.
5.6	Construct Validity of the Computational Measure
5.6.1	Correlation with IT Investment (ρ = 0.67)
The composite DT_Comp index shows a Pearson correlation of ρ = 0.67 with firm-level IT investment (when available from supplementary CSMAR data fields), confirming that text-based disclosure captures meaningful variation in actual technology resource allocation.
5.6.2	Correlation with Digital Patent Applications (ρ = 0.71)
The correlation with digital patent applications (patents in AI, software, and digital systems from CNRDS patent database) is ρ = 0.71, providing cross-validated evidence that keyword-based measures track genuine innovative activity rather than pure disclosure variation. 
5.6.3	Sensitivity Analysis Across Alternative Algorithmic Specifications
We report results for eight alternative operationalizations of the dependent variable (Table 6-3 in Chapter 6), including: (i) alternative composite aggregations (DT_Comp_B); (ii) application-domain sub-indices (DT_App_A, DT_App_B); (iii) technology-specific measures (AI_Tech, BigData_Tech, Cloud_Tech, Blockchain_Tech); (iv) binary and standardized variants; and (v) mention frequency versus intensity measures.
5.7	Disclosure Verbosity and Strategic Disclosure Bias
5.7.1	Normalized Measures: Keyword Counts Scaled by Report Length
We address disclosure verbosity concerns by constructing normalized measures where raw keyword counts are scaled by total disclosure character count (keywords per 1,000 disclosure characters). This normalization eliminates the mechanical correlation between overall report length and keyword frequency.
5.7.2	Direct Controls for Disclosure Length in Regression Specifications
As an alternative approach, we augment the baseline DID specification (Equation 4-1) with a direct control for disclosure length: log(1 + total disclosure characters), capturing overall verbosity at the firm-year level.
5.7.3	Residual Limitations and Scope of the Computational Approach
We recognize that text-based measures capture how openly firms disclose rather than their actual digital investments. After MSCI inclusion, firms might strategically use digital-related language to attract international investors without making real operational changes. 6.3.2	Placebo Test — Distribution of 1,000 Randomized Treatment Assignments
A standard concern with quasi-experimental designs is that an estimated treatment effect may reflect a spurious correlation arising from sample construction or model specification rather than a genuine causal mechanism. In this exercise, MSCI inclusion status is shuffled 1,000 times among non-treated firms and Equation (4-1) is re-estimated on each artificial assignment, producing an empirical null distribution of placebo coefficients. If the actual treatment estimate is drawn from a genuinely distinctive data-generating process, it should occupy an extreme position in this distribution, well beyond what arises under random assignment.

6.4.4	Heterogeneity Analysis Based on Firm Characteristics
Across firm-characteristic dimensions, MSCI effects are stronger for: (i) financially constrained firms (higher KZ index at baseline), consistent with larger marginal value of international capital access; (ii) firms with weaker pre-treatment governance (lower independent director ratio), reflecting greater room for governance improvement; and (iii) firms with lower pre-treatment digital transformation scores, consistent with convergence dynamics. These patterns collectively point to the financing and governance channels as the primary drivers of cross-firm variation in treatment effects. They also indicate that liberalization does not simply reward already-advanced firms; under certain conditions, it can generate catch-up dynamics among firms that were initially resource-constrained or organizationally lagging. This distributional nuance is important for policy design because it implies that complementary institutional support can amplify inclusive digital gains.

6.6	Discussion
This section integrates the empirical evidence into a coherent interpretive framework and evaluates its implications for theory, method, and policy. Rather than treating baseline coefficients, heterogeneity patterns, and mechanism estimates as separate results, the discussion synthesizes them as mutually reinforcing components of a single causal narrative. This integration is important for external validity: if multiple empirical lenses converge on the same directional conclusion, confidence in substantive interpretation is materially strengthened.
6.6.1	Interpretation of Main Findings in Theoretical Context
The 0.394 log-point preferred estimate indicates that MSCI inclusion increases corporate digital transformation by approximately 48%. This magnitude is economically significant — corresponding to half a within-firm standard deviation and a multiple of the control group's 4% organic annual growth rate — and directionally consistent with the theoretical framework in Chapter 2. The mechanism evidence in Section 6.5 provides direct support for financing (2/3 proxies significant) and governance (3/3) channels.
The heterogeneity patterns — stronger effects in eastern provinces, technology-intensive industries, and high-end manufacturing subsectors — are consistent with an absorptive capacity framework: international capital and knowledge flows generate the largest digital transformation returns when complementary institutional, human capital, and technological resources are already in place. 
6.6.2	Implications for the Capital Market Liberalization Literature
This study extends the capital market liberalization literature in three ways. First, it documents effects on corporate innovation behavior beyond financial performance metrics, connecting financial integration to real technology adoption. Second, it provides direct mechanism evidence at the firm level for financing and governance channels under a common identification strategy. Third, it demonstrates that liberalization effects are heterogeneous in economically meaningful ways, challenging uniform policy prescriptions. Conceptually, this shifts the liberalization debate from a narrow focus on allocative efficiency and valuation to a broader perspective in which capital-market opening can reshape firm capability accumulation and strategic investment horizons. Empirically, the findings support a conditional liberalization view: gains are strongest where institutional and organizational complements are sufficiently developed.
6.6.3	Implications for the Digital Transformation and Computational Economics Literature
By integrating validated text-as-data measures with a well-identified quasi-experiment, this study demonstrates the feasibility of causal inference about digital transformation drivers at scale in emerging markets. The consistent findings across eight alternative outcome measures validate the computational text analysis methodology as a reliable tool for studying corporate innovation. The approach provides a template for comparable studies across other emerging markets with mandatory annual report disclosure requirements. Methodologically, the contribution is twofold: it shows that disclosure-derived indices can be used in high-rigor causal designs when accompanied by normalization and placebo diagnostics, and it illustrates how modern DID concerns about heterogeneous treatment timing can be incorporated into a practical empirical workflow.
6.6.4	Policy Implications for Regulators, Firms, and International Investors
Several policy implications emerge. For policymakers, carefully sequenced capital market liberalization, as exemplified by progressive MSCI inclusion, can produce technology-upgrading spillovers in addition to conventional market-efficiency gains. At the same time, the heterogeneity evidence indicates that liberalization alone is insufficient: complementary investments in digital infrastructure, managerial capability, and regional institutional quality are required to prevent divergence in transformation outcomes. For corporate decision-makers, the findings suggest that capital-access benefits are magnified when firms pair financing strategy with governance reform, transparent disclosure, and credible long-horizon digital planning. For international investors, results indicate that stewardship and monitoring functions are not neutral side effects of capital flows; they can be active channels through which portfolio allocation influences real firm behavior.

 
Chapter 7	Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the core empirical contributions, clarifies the study's boundary conditions, and outlines a forward research agenda. It is organized to move from validated findings to interpretation, then from interpretation to remaining uncertainty. This structure aims to provide a balanced conclusion that reflects both the strength of the evidence and the limits of current identification and measurement.
The conclusion also positions the study within a broader research and policy trajectory. It distills what can be learned from the MSCI inclusion episode for emerging-market development strategy while clearly distinguishing robust evidence from open questions requiring further data and methods. By combining synthesis with transparency about uncertainty, the chapter seeks to offer findings that are actionable for scholars and policymakers without overstating causal reach beyond the observed institutional setting.
7.1	Summary of Main Findings
Using a difference-in-differences design on 35,264 firm-year observations (2010–2022) and validated NLP-based digital transformation measures, this thesis documents that the phased inclusion of Chinese A-share firms in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index is associated with a 0.394 log-point (≈ 48%) increase in digital transformation intensity. The effect is: (i) statistically significant at the 1% level in the preferred specification; (ii) robust across eight alternative outcome measures, PSM-DID, staggered-adoption diagnostics, disclosure-length controls, and multiple falsification tests; (iii) underpinned by credible pre-treatment parallel trends validated through event-study designs and 1,000-iteration placebo tests; and (iv) supported by direct mechanism evidence in financing and governance channels.
Heterogeneity analysis establishes that effects are strongest among firms in eastern provinces (≈ 126% of control mean), technology-intensive industries (Instrumentation: 28.130***; Electrical Equipment: 13.032***), and high-end manufacturing subcategories — patterns consistent with an absorptive capacity moderation framework.
Taken together, these results suggest that capital market liberalization in emerging economies can operate as a catalyst for technological upgrading when financial openness is matched by governance quality and organizational absorptive capacity. The evidence does not support a purely financial interpretation of inclusion effects; instead, it supports a combined financing-and-governance mechanism in the directly tested baseline.
7.2	Contributions 
1. Causal identification of digital transformation drivers: This thesis provides quasi-experimental evidence that international financial integration can causally increase firm-level digital transformation intensity in an emerging market context, using a large panel and multiple identification diagnostics.
2. Integration of text-as-data with causal econometrics: The study shows that validated NLP-based disclosure measures can be embedded in rigorous DID workflows when accompanied by normalization, placebo testing, and alternative-outcome sensitivity checks.
3. Unified mechanism architecture: Instead of testing channels separately, the thesis evaluates financing and governance pathways under a common treatment design, generating internally coherent evidence across six directly tested proxy variables.
4. Distributional interpretation of policy effects: By mapping heterogeneity across region, industry, and firm characteristics, the study moves beyond average effects and identifies where liberalization yields the strongest digital dividends and where complementary policy support is most needed.
Collectively, these contributions are designed to be mutually reinforcing: stronger identification supports mechanism interpretation, mechanism evidence improves policy relevance, and measurement transparency increases replicability and external credibility. This integrated contribution logic is central to the thesis's claim that capital market opening can be evaluated not only as a financial reform, but also as an institutional lever for firm-level technological upgrading.

7.3	Limitations  
Three limitations merit acknowledgment. First, the text-based outcome captures disclosed digital orientation and activity intensity rather than directly observed technology expenditures; although normalization and robustness checks reduce this concern, strategic disclosure bias cannot be fully eliminated. Second, the available post-treatment horizon, while sufficient for medium-run dynamics, remains too short to identify long-run equilibrium effects on productivity, profitability, or market structure. Third, although staggered diagnostics are reported, baseline TWFE estimates can still be sensitive to treatment-effect heterogeneity across cohorts, as highlighted in recent DID research. Fourth, direct mediator variables required for the knowledge-spillover channel (analyst coverage and foreign institutional ownership) are not yet integrated into the current merged panel, so knowledge-channel mediation is not directly estimated in the baseline.
7.4	Future Work 
Future research should proceed along five directions. First, mechanism identification should extend the baseline by integrating direct knowledge-transfer mediators and estimating the chain MSCI inclusion → analyst coverage/foreign ownership → digital transformation. Second, heterogeneity-robust DID estimators and imputation-based event-study approaches should be implemented as primary estimators when longer post-treatment windows and larger cohort samples become available. Third, cross-country comparative designs exploiting analogous benchmark-inclusion episodes can clarify which findings are China-specific and which generalize across emerging markets. Fourth, extending the sample beyond 2022 will allow testing whether estimated effects persist, plateau, or reverse as inclusion factors and global portfolio weights evolve. Fifth, richer NLP methods such as supervised classification, contextual embeddings, and topic evolution models can improve construct validity by distinguishing strategic rhetoric from operational implementation in corporate digital narratives.
